In the spirit of the game:
Application of game theory in sports conflicts

Utpal Shetty

What is this all about ?

Game theory revolves around the analysis of how decision makers make decisions with or without the knowledge of reactions and choices of other decision makers. A game in general is defined as any interaction between two or more people, where the outcomes of interaction depends on what everybody does and everybody has different levels of happiness for different outcomes.

In the recently concluded 19th Asian games, that took place in Hangzhou, China, 483 events in 40 sports took place. One among them was Kabaddi. In the final of Men's kabaddi between India and Iran, with 1 minute and 5 seconds remaining on the clock, there was a conflict between the two teams regarding the points that has to be given to an Indian raider for a particular raid. The decision was to be made by referees but neither of the two parties seemed to agree on a neutral solution. So this is an attempt to analyze this game based on game theory.

What actually happened ?

In the above mentioned game, both the teams were tied at 28 points each (the actual match points may vary). The raider from the Indian team goes to raid, at this point, one Iranian defender steps out of bonds (which lends one point to the India) and the Indian raider goes into the lobby without touching the opponent (which lends one point to Iran). Following the raider, three more Iranian players went into the lobby (this is where the confusion starts, whether to lend three points to India or not). Now due to rule changes in Pro-Kabaddi league (professional league in India) that took place few months earlier, there was a possibility that those rules were not applied to Asian games. Due to the confusion among officials and referees, regarding which rule to follow, they were stuck in between an altercation, where the Indians were not satisfied with 1-1 ruling of the officials. (The exact rules and subsequent reason for demand of points by each of the involved parties are not of interest here, rather we are focused on their demands.)

Now when we look closely in the above game, one might observe that the dominant strategy i.e., the one strategy that gives the player an upperhand in terms of payoff is accepting the decision given by referees, irrespective of what IRAN does.

But in real-life sports conflicts, the decision might not be so easy to take. Partly, because more often than not, in such conflicts, both the parties would be demanding certain payoff or results.

In this particular scenario, team INDIA would be demanding 4:1 in their favour, whereas team IRAN would be demanding 1:1 each. This conflict, then can be viewed through the lens of an extensive form game, whose induced normal form (payoff matrix) is given as:

IRAN
Aa Ar Ra Rr
INDIA Aa 1 , 1 1 , 1 1 , 0 1 , 0
Ar 1 , 1 1 , 1 1 , 0 1 , 0
Ra 0 , 1 0 , 1 4 , 1 4 , 0
Rr 0 , 1 0 , 1 0 , 1 0 , 0

How do we interpret these actions and their payoff ??

Before we delve into interpretation of the actions and their payoffs, we need to look at the tree representation of this extensive game.



Here, if INDIA plays the strategy of Ra and IRAN plays Rr then we reach the payoff of (4 , 0) using the game tree, as follows:

The R in Ra refers to the action taken by INDIA in the first decision node followed by a in the second decision node. Similarly, for IRAN , R in the first decision node for them and r in the subsequent node. Thus, reaching the payoff of (4 , 0).

If they had had both considered taking Aa instead, the extensive game would have finished with their first actions itself, because after playing A in their first decision nodes, they would reach a payoff of (1 , 1) as per the game tree.
